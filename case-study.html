Case Study

Designed a clinical alert dashboard to reduce override of critical drug-interaction warnings

In a study of 18,354 medication orders across two hospitals, physicians overrode 95% of drug-interaction alerts — with no evidence of improvement since the Meaningful Use era began. (Bryant et al., Applied Clinical Informatics, 2014)

Usability testing is in progress — Round 2 wraps in early August, with final results and the full outcome story publishing here by Aug 15, 2026.

Role

Solo — research, synthesis, design, testing, prototyping

Timeline

May–Aug 2026

Tools

Figma, Stark, Maze, Claude API

30-second summary

  • Problem: hospitalists override 95% of AI drug-interaction alerts because every alert looks the same regardless of severity.
  • Approach: synthesized 18 clinical sources (no primary access to hospitalists), then designed and tested with 5 proxy clinical testers.
  • Key decision: a triage table with ESI severity tiers, AI confidence scores, and friction that scales with the risk of the action.
  • Status: usability testing in progress — results land here once collected.

A note on method: I didn't have access to practicing hospitalists for generative interviews — a gated clinical domain, out of reach for a solo junior researcher. The persona and problem frame are built from 18 peer-reviewed and expert sources instead; evaluative testing (Maze + SUS + NASA-TLX) runs with proxy testers — RNs, a nursing student, and a hospital administrator — to still validate against people with real clinical workflow exposure.

How alert fatigue breaks clinical decision-making

Hospitalists manage the highest volume of daily EHR medication orders in inpatient care and are the primary recipients of drug-interaction alerts — but every alert presents at identical visual priority regardless of severity, with no positional, size, or color signal distinguishing a life-threatening interaction from a routine advisory.

  • A 2015 UCSF case: a 16-year-old received a 39-fold antibiotic overdose after both a physician and pharmacist overrode a critical alert that "looked exactly the same" as routine ones. A Boston Globe investigation tied at least 216 U.S. deaths (2005–2010) to alarm fatigue. (R1-003)
  • The Joint Commission issued an urgent alarm-safety directive in 2013 (updated 2016); the ECRI Institute named alarm-related problems the top health-technology hazard for four consecutive years. (R1-003, R1-004)
  • UCSF ICU bedside monitors alone generated 187 alerts per patient per day — one every eight minutes, almost all clinically insignificant. Across five ICUs in one month: 2,507,822 unique alarms. (R1-005)
  • Each interruption during medication administration raises error risk by ~13%; four interruptions doubles the rate of errors likely to cause permanent harm or death. (R1-003, R1-006)

The design implication: the problem isn't alert volume alone — uniform visual design makes every alert look equivalent, training clinicians to ignore all of them. A 95% override rate where critical and routine alerts are visually indistinguishable is a predictable outcome, not a user failure.

Three questions this design had to answer

  1. What is the minimum information — urgency, confidence, recommended action — a hospitalist needs to triage an alert without leaving the alert view?
  2. What visual or structural signal distinguishes a critical alert from a routine one at a glance, before the clinician reads any text?
  3. Under what conditions does an AI confidence score increase a hospitalist's willingness to act on an alert rather than override it?

What the research surfaced

  • Uniform visual priority is the proximate cause of mass override. No preattentive signal — position, size, or reliable color — separates a critical interaction from a routine advisory before the clinician reads it. The 95.1% drug-drug and 91.2% drug-allergy override rates are the measurable outcome of that design failure, not clinician negligence. (R1-001, R1-002, R1-003)
  • AI confidence display is the single largest lever on clinician acceptance — larger than model accuracy itself. High-confidence AI predictions (90–99%) were overridden 1.7% of the time; low-confidence predictions (70–79%) were overridden 99.3% of the time, across 6,689 cardiovascular cases. (R1-011) Framing matters too: a professional, analytical presentation outperforms a warm, conversational one. (R1-017)
  • The information needed to decide isn't where the decision is made. Expert consensus specifies drug identity, severity tier, and recommended action must appear in the alert header itself, not behind navigation — one EHR that did this measured significantly lower clinician workload. (R1-007, R1-009)
  • Severity tiering and role-tailoring are the two interventions proven to work — more than any cosmetic visual change. A systematic review of 2007–2017 literature found role-tailoring the single most effective structural fix, severity tiering the second. (R1-008)

Design decisions, and what they're traced to

D1-003 — Triage table, not a card list or zoned dashboard

Each pending alert is a table row — severity tier, drug ordered vs. drug on file, AI confidence, and recommended action all visible without expanding, sorted by severity. Two alternatives were ruled out: a card list (critical cards can scroll off-screen) and a severity-zoned dashboard (breaks down with zero critical alerts). Tables won because they're native to how clinicians already read labs and medication lists, and the only format that supports comparing multiple patients at a glance.

Mazur et al. 2019 (R1-009); NN/g 2018 (R1-013)

D1-004 — Severity tiers use ESI, not an invented scale

The alert panel borrows the Emergency Severity Index — the triage standard already in a hospitalist's vocabulary — rather than a made-up "High/Medium/Low." "ESI-1" communicates urgency with zero explanation needed; an invented label would require the clinician to learn a new scale mid-shift.

The Joint Commission 2013/2016 (R1-004); Hussain et al. 2019 (R1-008)

D1-005 — Color reinforces severity, it never carries it alone

Each ESI tier gets one accent color on the row's border and badge — but strip the color out and the ESI-1 row is still identifiable by position, height, and weight. Deliberate accessibility call: color-only encoding fails for color-deficient clinicians and is unreliable under time pressure regardless. AI confidence text stays neutral at every severity level, so a red badge never gets confused with "this score can't be trusted."

NN/g 2018 (R1-013); The Joint Commission 2013/2016 (R1-004)

D1-006 / D1-007 — Friction scales with the risk of the action

A recommended action (Hold) completes in one click. Overriding requires selecting a reason from five visible radio options — not a dropdown — before Confirm enables, with nothing pre-selected. This fixes a documented EHR failure mode: dropdown override-reasons are too coarse, so clinicians pick the fastest option instead of the accurate one, destroying the data's value for safety review.

Payne et al. 2015 (R1-007)

D1-002 — A tool-schema field name was quietly reversing clinical meaning

Building the explainability panel meant using the Claude API to extract structured alert fields from clinical text. The first schema version labeled fields drug_primary / drug_interacting — given a Warfarin + Ciprofloxacin case, the model labeled Ciprofloxacin as drug_primary and Warfarin as drug_interacting, reversed from a prescribing point of view. Renamed to drug_ordered / drug_on_file — self-explanatory without inference, and a reminder that schema field names shape how a UI ends up labeling a model's output.

Payne et al. 2015 (R1-007)

What I learned

Failure 1 — a schema field name almost shipped a clinically inverted label. The drug_primary / drug_interacting naming (see D1-002 above) wasn't caught by looking at the UI — it surfaced only when I traced the model's actual output against the clinical scenario and asked which drug a hospitalist would expect to see first. Consequence if unshipped: the exact panel meant to reduce override-by-confusion would have introduced a new, subtler version of the same confusion — reversed drug context, at the moment a clinician is deciding whether to trust the AI. Lesson: schema and copy review needs the same scrutiny as visual review, against a real clinical scenario, not an abstract field list.
