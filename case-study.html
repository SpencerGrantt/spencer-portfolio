Case Study

Designed a clinical alert dashboard to reduce override of critical drug-interaction warnings

In a study of 18,354 medication orders across two hospitals, physicians overrode 95% of drug-interaction alerts — with no evidence of improvement since the Meaningful Use era began. (Bryant et al., Applied Clinical Informatics, 2014)

How alert fatigue breaks clinical decision-making

Hospitalists manage the highest volume of daily EHR medication orders in inpatient care and are the primary recipients of drug-interaction alerts. Every alert in a modern EHR presents at identical visual priority regardless of severity — no positional hierarchy, no size differentiation, no reliable color signal distinguishes a life-threatening interaction from a routine advisory before the clinician reads the full alert text.

The cost of that flat hierarchy is not abstract. In 2015, a 16-year-old at UCSF received a 39-fold antibiotic overdose after both a physician and pharmacist overrode a critical alert — each later reporting that it looked identical to the dozens of routine alerts they dismissed daily. The prescribing physician's own words: "There is no difference between a minuscule overdose and a very large overdose. They all look exactly the same." A Boston Globe investigation tied at least 216 U.S. deaths between 2005–2010 to alarm fatigue. (R1-003)

It's also a compliance exposure, not just a clinical one: The Joint Commission issued an urgent directive on alarm safety in 2013 (updated 2016), and the ECRI Institute named alarm-related problems the top health-technology hazard for four consecutive years. (R1-003, R1-004)

And it's a volume problem before it's a design problem: UCSF ICU bedside monitors alone generated 187 audible alerts per patient per day — one every eight minutes, the overwhelming majority clinically insignificant. Across five ICUs in one month: 2,507,822 unique alarms. (R1-005) Each interruption during medication administration raises error risk by ~13%; four interruptions doubles the rate of errors likely to cause permanent harm or death. (R1-003, R1-006)

The design implication: the problem isn't alert volume alone — it's that uniform visual design makes every alert look equivalent, training clinicians to ignore all of them. A 95% override rate in a system where critical and routine alerts are visually indistinguishable is a predictable outcome, not a user failure.

Three questions this design had to answer

  1. What is the minimum information — urgency, confidence, recommended action — a hospitalist needs to triage an alert without leaving the alert view?
  2. What visual or structural signal distinguishes a critical alert from a routine one at a glance, before the clinician reads any text?
  3. Under what conditions does an AI confidence score increase a hospitalist's willingness to act on an alert rather than override it?

Why secondary research, and what it means for this study

I didn't have access to practicing hospitalists for generative interviews — a gated clinical domain, out of reach for a solo junior researcher. The persona and problem frame are instead built from 18 peer-reviewed and expert sources, trading primary interviews I couldn't run for depth of secondary research. Evaluative testing (two rounds, Maze + SUS + NASA-TLX) was run with proxy testers — RNs, a nursing student, and a hospital administrator who oversees nursing staff — to still validate the design against people with real clinical workflow exposure. Stated as a limitation, not hidden.

What the research surfaced

Uniform visual priority is the proximate cause of mass override. No preattentive signal — no position, size, or reliable color — separates a critical interaction from a routine advisory before the clinician reads it. The 95.1% drug-drug override rate and 91.2% drug-allergy override rate are the measurable outcome of that design failure, not clinician negligence. (R1-001, R1-002, R1-003)

AI confidence display is the single largest lever on clinician acceptance — larger than model accuracy itself. In a study of 6,689 cardiovascular cases, high-confidence AI predictions (90–99% band) were overridden 1.7% of the time; low-confidence predictions (70–79% band) were overridden 99.3% of the time. (R1-011) How the AI is framed matters too: perceived AI intelligence correlates with following its recommendations (B = 0.31, p < .001); perceived AI emotion correlates negatively (B = –1.04, p = .042) — a professional, analytical presentation outperforms a warm, conversational one. (R1-017)

The information needed to decide isn't where the decision is made. Expert consensus specifies drug identity, severity tier, and recommended action must appear in the alert header itself — not behind navigation. When one EHR surfaced this in the primary view, physicians showed significantly lower NASA-TLX workload and managed more abnormal results simultaneously. (R1-007, R1-009)

Severity tiering and role-tailoring are the two interventions proven to work — more than any cosmetic visual change. A systematic review of 2007–2017 literature found role-tailoring alerts to the right clinician the single most effective structural fix, severity tiering the second. (R1-008)

Design decisions, and what they're traced to

D1-003 — Triage table, not a card list or zoned dashboard

Each pending alert is a table row — severity tier, drug ordered vs. drug on file, AI confidence, recommended action all visible without expanding. Rows sort by severity; the top row carries the heaviest visual weight. Two alternatives were built and ruled out: a size-dominant card list (scales badly past a few alerts, critical cards can scroll off-screen) and a severity-zoned dashboard (breaks down when no critical alerts exist, and isn't a layout clinicians already know). The tabular format won because it's native to how clinicians already read labs, vitals, and medication lists — and it's the only format that supports comparing multiple patients at a glance under time pressure.

Mazur et al. 2019 (R1-009); NN/g 2018 (R1-013)

D1-004 — Severity tiers use ESI, not an invented scale

The alert panel borrows the Emergency Severity Index — the triage standard already in a hospitalist's vocabulary — rather than a made-up "High/Medium/Low." "ESI-1" communicates urgency with zero explanation needed; an invented label would require the clinician to learn a new scale mid-shift.

The Joint Commission 2013/2016 (R1-004); Hussain et al. 2019 (R1-008)

D1-005 — Color reinforces severity, it never carries it alone

Each ESI tier gets one accent color on the row's left border and badge — but strip the color out and the ESI-1 row is still identifiable by position (top), height (tallest), and type weight (boldest). Deliberate accessibility decision: color-only encoding fails for color-deficient clinicians and is unreliable under time pressure regardless. AI confidence text stays neutral dark at every severity level — confidence signals reliability, not danger, and coloring it red would conflate "this alert is critical" with "this score can't be trusted."

NN/g 2018 (R1-013); The Joint Commission 2013/2016 (R1-004)

D1-006 / D1-007 — Friction scales with the risk of the action

A recommended action (Hold) completes in one click, zero friction. Overriding an alert requires selecting a reason from five visible radio options — not a dropdown — before the Confirm button enables; nothing is pre-selected, so the clinician has to make an active choice. This directly answers a documented EHR failure mode: override-reason dropdowns are too coarse, so clinicians pick the fastest option rather than the accurate one, destroying the signal value of override data for retrospective safety review.

Payne et al. 2015 (R1-007)

D1-002 — A tool-schema field name was quietly reversing clinical meaning

Building the explainability panel meant using the Claude API to extract structured alert fields from clinical text. The first schema version labeled fields drug_primary / drug_interacting. Given a Warfarin + Ciprofloxacin case, the model labeled Ciprofloxacin (the new order triggering the alert) as drug_primary and Warfarin (the existing active order) as drug_interacting — technically reversed from a prescribing point of view, and exactly the ambiguity expert guidance says alert headers must not require a clinician to resolve. Renamed to drug_ordered / drug_on_file — self-explanatory without inference, and a reminder that schema field names shape both what a model outputs and how a UI ends up labeling it.

Payne et al. 2015 (R1-007)

What I learned

Failure 1 — a schema field name almost shipped a clinically inverted label. The drug_primary / drug_interacting naming (see D1-002 above) wasn't caught by looking at the UI — it surfaced only when I traced the model's actual output against the clinical scenario and asked which drug a hospitalist would expect to see first. Consequence if unshipped: the exact panel meant to reduce override-by-confusion would have introduced a new, subtler version of the same confusion — reversed drug context, at the moment a clinician is deciding whether to trust the AI. Lesson: schema and copy review needs the same scrutiny as visual review, against a real clinical scenario, not an abstract field list.
